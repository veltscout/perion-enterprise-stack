This technical manual provides the engineering specifications for the Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure, a decentralized, low-voltage linear architecture. The document presents quantitative data and design rules. Distributed linear illumination eliminates high-contrast shadows. The system carries an IP69K rating and rodent-resistant cabling. Power consumption is reduced by 80 %. Heavy civil infrastructure requirements are removed. The document is the authoritative guide for specifiers to implement a safe, integrated, and long-lasting illumination solution that functions as both an illuminator and a Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor.

1.1 Executive Summary

The Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure is a decentralized, low-voltage, linear architecture. This document is the Level 2 Technical and Engineering Authority. It supplies the depth required for consulting engineers, specifiers, and procurement specialists to evaluate system performance, durability, and integration capabilities within infrastructure projects.

Traditional perimeter illumination relies on high-wattage HID or LED floodlights mounted on tall masts. These create high-contrast environments characterized by intense hot spots and deep shadow pockets. These environments are incompatible with the dynamic range limitations of modern digital CCTV sensors and the edge-detection requirements of AI-driven video analytics. The Perion system distributes low-wattage illumination directly onto the fence line. A continuous wall of light with high uniformity ratio is created. Consistent visibility is ensured for human observation and automated surveillance.

Operating on a 24 V DC Safety Extra-Low Voltage (SELV) framework, the system eliminates the extensive civil infrastructure required by legacy systems. This includes deep trenching, concrete foundations, and high-voltage cabling. This architectural efficiency results in an approximate 80 % reduction in power consumption and a total project cost lower than traditional high-voltage alternatives. With an ingress protection rating of IP69K and a 100,000 Hours (L70) rated lifespan, the Perion system is engineered for resilience in the most demanding industrial, mining, and agricultural environments.

2.1 System Architecture and Design Authority

The Perion system architecture is a linear, distributed model for perimeter illumination infrastructure. It follows the geometry of the fence or wall to eliminate shadows and provide uniform illumination. This section defines the engineering logic and component roles that form the basis of the system's performance.

2.1.1 Core Architecture and Topologies

The system is centered around the Perion Power Pack, which serves as the primary regulation and control hub. From this hub, the Illumination Cable — a proprietary, UV-stabilized backbone — carries 24 V DC power along the perimeter. The LED modules are attached directly to this cable using Quick Connect technology, which facilitates a gas-tight, waterproof connection without the need for pre-set junction points or specialized labor.

Topology Type Engineering Application Key Benefit
Single-Supply Perimeters up to 1,000 m with moderate lighting requirements Minimal hardware footprint and centralized control.
Multi-Supply Extended perimeters (>1 km) or sites requiring segmentation Operational resilience; a failure in one sector does not impact others.
Dual-Feed (Loop) Long runs where voltage drop must be mitigated for consistency Ensures the furthest LED modules receive nominal voltage to maintain lux levels.
Solar/Hybrid Off-grid locations or areas with frequent grid instability Solar-native architecture allows for direct battery/solar integration without inverters.

The system supports multiple topologies to accommodate different site layouts and power requirements. For infrastructure, the segmented multi-supply approach is used, as it allows personnel to locate events with greater geographical precision.

2.1.2 Component Engineering and Material Selection

Each component of the Perion system is selected to withstand extreme environmental exposure while maintaining electrical efficiency. The illumination cable is an engineered asset featuring a rodent-repellent formulation designed to prevent biological damage, a common failure mode in agricultural and industrial settings.

2.1.3 Engineering Consideration: Thermal Management and Lifespan

The 3 W LED modules are housed in industrial-grade, UV-stable polymers that provide passive thermal dissipation. Because the total power is distributed across many low-wattage points rather than concentrated in a few high-wattage fixtures, the system avoids the thermal stress common in high-output floodlights. This thermal efficiency is the primary driver behind the 100,000 Hours (L70) rated lifespan, which translates to approximately 22 years of service on a standard dusk-to-dawn cycle.

3.1.5 FAQ: Technical Specifications

Frequently Asked Questions — Technical Specifications

Q: Why is the IP69K rating important for perimeter illumination?

Standard outdoor lighting is typically IP65 or IP66, which protects against rain. In industrial or mining environments, equipment is cleaned with high-pressure washers. IP69K ensures that high-pressure, high-temperature water jets will not penetrate the LED module, preventing internal corrosion and failure.

Q: Does the system require a master electrician for installation?

Because the system operates on 24 V DC Safety Extra-Low Voltage (SELV), the lighting runs themselves generally do not require a master electrician. The connection of the Power Pack to the 220 V AC mains must be performed by a qualified professional according to local regulations.

Q: Can the system be used in saline or coastal environments?

Yes. The industrial polymers used in the modules and the gas-tight nature of the Quick Connect technology provide resistance to salt-spray corrosion, which typically degrades traditional metal-housed floodlights.

4.1 System Design Rules and Engineering Calculations

Design authority for the Perion system rests on maintaining electrical stability and photometric uniformity over the length of the perimeter. This section details the logic required to design a high-performance lighting layout.

4.1.1 Voltage Drop Logic and Calculations

In 24 V DC systems, voltage drop is the primary constraint governing maximum run lengths. To ensure that the LED modules at the end of a run operate at their designed efficiency and brightness, the system must be engineered to minimize resistive losses.

The standard formula for calculating voltage drop in the Perion system is:

ΔV = 2 × L × I × (R / 1000)

Where:

  • ΔV — Voltage drop in Volts (V).
  • L — One-way cable length in meters (m).
  • I — Current in Amperes (A).
  • R — Conductor resistance in Ohms per kilometer (Ω/km).

Engineering Constraints

  • Maximum Allowable Drop: 10 % (2.4 V drop from 24 V nominal).
  • Design Target: 7 % (to provide a buffer for environmental factors and connection resistance).
  • Typical Current Draw: Each 3 W module draws approximately 0.125 A at 24 V.

4.1.2 Dual-Feed Topology and Redundancy Strategies

For perimeters where a single feed exceeds the 10 % voltage drop limit, a dual-feed topology is specified. By providing power from both ends of the cable or splitting the run into multiple sectors, the total resistance is halved, and voltage stability is maintained across the entire length. This strategy is required for achieving the maintained illuminance target of 10 Lux on runs approaching 1,000 m.

4.1.4 FAQ: Design Rules

Frequently Asked Questions — Design Rules

Q: How do I handle corners and gates in my design?

For corners, it is recommended to reduce spacing (e.g., to 2 m) to compensate for the change in geometry and ensure no shadows are cast. For gates, additional modules can be added to the gate leaves themselves, as the low-voltage cable is flexible and durable enough to handle standard gate operation.

Q: Can I use a single Power Pack for a 1.2 km perimeter?

A single Power Pack is rated for up to 1,000 m. For a 1.2 km perimeter, engineering best practice dictates using two Power Packs to segment the perimeter into two 600 m runs. This ensures voltage compliance and provides redundancy.

5.1 CCTV Integration and Illumination Optimization

The Perion system is engineered to provide the optimal environment for electronic monitoring, human recognition, and AI-driven video analytics.

5.1.1 Optimizing the CCTV Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

Digital camera sensors struggle with high-contrast scenes. When a traditional floodlight creates a hot spot, the camera's auto-iris closes to protect the sensor, which turns the surrounding unlit areas into pitch black. This is the iris clamp effect. Perion eliminates this by providing uniform, low-glare illumination that allows the camera sensor to operate at a consistent exposure level, maximizing the signal-to-noise ratio and reducing the graininess typical of low-light video.

5.1.2 Support for AI and Video Analytics (YOLOv8)

AI classification algorithms, such as those using YOLOv8, require clear edge detection and consistent pixel contrast to identify human or vehicular targets accurately. High-uniformity lighting (Uniformity Ratio 0.4 – 0.6) ensures that target features remain visible as they move across the camera's field of view, reducing false negatives and maintaining a high confidence score for the analytic software.

5.1.3 Field of View (FOV) and Mounting Heights

The mounting height and orientation of the lighting are critical for maximizing the effectiveness of the monitoring system. Lighting should be installed parallel to the fence line, facing inward toward the monitored area to illuminate the approach and provide a clear silhouette for cameras.

  • Recommended Mounting Height: 2.5 m to 3.0 m.
  • Shadow Management: The linear architecture uses overlapping beam patterns to eliminate point-source shadowing, ensuring that an intruder cannot hide behind vertical fence posts or other structural elements.

5.1.4 Perimeter-Lock Cable-Continuity Monitoring

The Perion system functions as both an illuminator and a sensor. The illumination cable is monitored for continuity by the Power Pack. If the cable is cut or power is disabled, the system immediately detects the break and triggers a dry-contact relay. This relay can be integrated into the site's alarm panel, Video Management System (VMS), or Security Management System (SMS) to trigger an immediate response.

5.1.5 FAQ: CCTV and Integration

Frequently Asked Questions — CCTV and Integration

Q: Does Perion work with Infrared (IR) cameras?

Yes. Perion is designed to provide white light that often keeps cameras in Day Mode longer. This allows for color recognition of clothing and vehicles, which is superior to black-and-white IR footage for investigative purposes.

Q: Can I integrate the Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor relay with my VMS?

Yes. Most VMS platforms accept digital inputs. When the Perion cable is cut, the relay can trigger the VMS to zoom a PTZ camera to that specific sector and alert the operator.

6.1.4 FAQ: Performance and Efficiency

Frequently Asked Questions — Performance and Efficiency

Q: How can 3 W modules provide enough light for a 600 m perimeter?

Uniformity, not raw power, determines effective illumination. By distributing the light every 4 m, a continuous curtain of light is created. A 400 W floodlight on a pole creates a bright spot, but most of its energy is wasted illuminating areas that the camera cannot see or that are too far away for effective recognition.

Q: What is the impact of load-shedding on the system?

Because Perion consumes 625 W for 600 m, it is straightforward to back up with small battery banks or solar arrays. This ensures 100 % uptime during power outages, unlike power-hungry HV systems that require massive generators or expensive UPS systems.

8.1 Installation and Commissioning

This section outlines the auditable procedures required for safe installation and verification of the Perion system.

8.1.1 Safety and Regulatory Compliance

Installation must follow local low-voltage electrical codes. Because the system is 24 V DC (SELV), it is inherently safer than AC systems, but installers must observe standard site safety protocols.

8.1.2 Step-by-Step Installation Procedure

  1. Site Assessment: Verify perimeter length and identify Power Pack mounting locations near AC mains or solar points.

  2. Power Pack Installation: Mount the Power Pack in a secure, weather-protected location.

  3. Cable Deployment: Run the illumination cable along the fence at the specified height (typically 2.5 m – 3.0 m). Secure the cable using UV-rated stainless steel or heavy-duty polymer ties at regular intervals.

  4. Module Attachment: Using the spacing plan (e.g., 4 m), attach the LED modules to the cable using the Quick Connect crimp technology. Ensure the module is oriented to face the desired detection zone.

  5. Termination: Apply the IP69K-rated termination kit to the end of the cable run to seal the circuit.

  6. Integration: Wire the Power Pack's dry-contact relay into the site's alarm or VMS panel.

8.1.3 Commissioning and Verification Tests

To ensure the system meets engineering specifications, the following commissioning tests must be documented:

  • Voltage Verification: Measure DC voltage at the furthest module; it must be ≥21.6 V (10 % max drop).
  • Lux Level Audit: Verify that light levels meet the design target (e.g., >10 Lux at 4 m spacing).
  • Perimeter-Lock Verification: Disconnect the cable at a junction to confirm the Power Pack triggers the alarm relay.
  • CCTV Image Audit: Confirm uniform illumination and lack of glare in the video management system at night.

8.1.4 FAQ: Installation and Commissioning

Frequently Asked Questions — Installation and Commissioning

Q: Can the cable be shortened in the field?

Yes. The illumination cable can be cut to any length up to its maximum rated capacity. The installer simply applies the termination kit to the new end-point to maintain the environmental seal.

Q: What happens if I accidentally reverse the polarity of the cable?

The Perion Power Pack and modules are designed with polarity protection. The system simply will not light up if the polarity is reversed, protecting the electronics from damage until the wiring is corrected.

Q: Is the system suitable for hazardous environments (Ex/ATEX)?

10.1 Conclusions and Final Engineering Recommendations

The technical analysis of the Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure confirms its effectiveness as a high-performance, cost-efficient, and resilient illumination infrastructure solution. By shifting the illumination model from high-voltage point-sources to low-voltage linear distribution, Perion addresses the fundamental limitations of traditional lighting in modern monitoring contexts.

For consulting engineers and specifiers, the following recommendations are provided based on the engineering data:

  1. Uniformity over Intensity: Prioritize the uniformity ratio (U) when designing perimeter illumination. High intensity is counter-productive if it creates high-contrast shadows that affect CCTV sensors.

  2. Leverage SELV Safety: Utilize the 24 V DC architecture to reduce civil costs, speed up deployment, and minimize site safety risks during installation and maintenance.

  3. Perimeter-Lock Integration: Treat the lighting as a sensor. Ensure that the Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor relay is fully integrated into the site's alarm and video management ecosystems to provide real-time alerts upon physical interference.

  4. Design for Longevity: Adhere to the 10 % voltage drop limit and utilize dual-feed topologies on long runs to maximize the 100,000 Hours (L70) service life of the LED modules.

The Perion system provides a technically irrefutable alternative for perimeter illumination, delivering operational outcomes with an 80 % reduction in energy usage and an order-of-magnitude reduction in total project cost. Its IP69K rating and rodent-repellent infrastructure make it the specification of record for critical infrastructure protection in the world's most demanding environments.
