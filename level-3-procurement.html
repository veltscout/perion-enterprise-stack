Execution Framework Abstract

This document establishes a formal execution framework for the procurement of the Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure, acting as a bridge between high-level engineering designs and physical asset deployment. The framework centres on the transition from viewing lighting as a simple commodity to treating it as an integrated sensing asset that offers significant financial advantages — including a 60 % to 70 % reduction in capital expenditure by eliminating traditional civil works.

Structured around a modular SKU catalogue and strict configuration logic, the framework enforces mandatory commercial safeguards — including rodent-repellent cabling and relays for the Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor — to mitigate risk and preserve the system's 5-year warranty. This guide serves as the authoritative procurement truth, providing the precise formulas, zoning rules, and spare-parts logistics required to ensure every installation maintains enterprise-grade reliability and auditability.

Executive Summary

Executive Commercial Architecture Summary

The Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure provides measurable advantages in critical infrastructure protection by transitioning from traditional commodity lighting to an integrated sensing asset and risk reduction layer. This commercial architecture is designed to integrate seamlessly into a 6-level documentation governance model, ensuring that the procurement process translates engineering intent (Level 2) into physical asset deployment with zero dilution of technical truth.

The primary commercial value proposition centres on the reduction of capital expenditure (CAPEX) through the elimination of traditional civil works. In linear perimeters under 2 km, the Perion system provides a documented 60 % to 70 % reduction in trenching and conduit requirements, as it utilises the existing fence infrastructure for mounting and distribution. From a procurement perspective, this transforms the project's financial profile by reallocating budget from high-cost civil labour to high-performance technology assets.

The system's architecture leverages low-voltage (24 V DC) distribution, which facilitates a "Plug-and-Play" or "Quick Connect" deployment model. This allows for installation by non-specialised labour or existing security maintenance teams, further optimising the total cost of ownership (TCO). However, to maintain the integrity of this infrastructure, procurement must strictly adhere to the Bill of Materials (BOM) rules and SKU-specific safeguards outlined in this document. Commercial risk is mitigated through the mandatory inclusion of relays for the Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor and the selection of UV-resistant, rodent-repellent cabling, which protects the asset's longevity in harsh industrial environments.

This framework serves as the authoritative guide for procurement committees, EPC contractors, and security architects to ensure that every Perion installation meets enterprise-grade standards for reliability, auditability, and performance.

Section 1

1. Hierarchy and Positioning in the Master System

1.1 The Role of Level 3 in Commercial Governance

Within the Perion 6-level documentation hierarchy, Level 3 serves as the critical junction between technical design and financial execution. It is the layer where abstract engineering requirements are codified into actionable purchasing data. The documentation hierarchy is structured to ensure that no single document carries the burden of all stakeholders, thereby preventing the "information bloat" that often leads to procurement errors.

Level 3 documentation focuses exclusively on the procurement, ordering, and BOM execution lifecycle. It does not attempt to redefine the engineering laws established in Level 2 (Technical & Engineering), nor does it engage in the brand-building exercises of Level 5 (Design & Brand). Instead, it provides the "Procurement Truth" — the definitive list of SKUs, ordering rules, and commercial safeguards required to deliver a functional system that satisfies the manufacturer's warranty and the client's requirements.

1.2 Inter-Document Traceability

The effectiveness of the Level 3 framework depends on its strict alignment with other levels in the documentation stack. Procurement officers and project managers must understand the following traceability links:

Section 2

2. Commercial Risk Controls & Ordering Safeguards

The procurement of perimeter infrastructure involves inherent risks related to asset longevity, installation quality, and operational reliability. The following safeguards are mandatory for all enterprise-grade Perion deployments to ensure risk mitigation throughout the asset lifecycle.

2.1 Asset Integrity and Perimeter-Lock Safeguards

A common risk in perimeter infrastructure is the physical tampering or theft of components. The Perion system mitigates this commercially by integrating the Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor. Procurement must ensure that every Power Pack is ordered with the corresponding Relay Interface Module (PPL-INT-RELAY) to enable third-party alarm integration.

Mandatory procurement risk controls — asset integrity and Perimeter-Lock safeguards.
Risk Category Mitigation Strategy Procurement Mandate
Cable Theft Real-time alarm triggering via the Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor. Mandatory inclusion of PPL-INT-RELAY per zone.
Vandalism Quick Connect modularity for rapid component replacement. Maintain a 5 % on-site spare buffer for LED modules.
Power Interruption Transition to solar-ready or battery-backed Power Packs. Use PPL-PP-24V-SOL for critical off-grid nodes.

2.2 Material Reliability and Environmental Safeguards

Commercial risk extends to the physical durability of the system. Traditional lighting cables often fail due to UV degradation or rodent damage, leading to high maintenance costs and operational gaps. The Perion procurement strategy mandates the use of PPL-CBL-UV-RR (UV-Resistant, Rodent-Repellent) cable to protect the long-term ROI.

  • UV Resistance: Ensures the cable jacket does not become brittle and crack under extreme solar exposure, maintaining the IP69K ingress protection of the overall system.
  • Rodent Repellency: Incorporates specialised chemical deterrents within the cable jacket to prevent "gnaw-through" failures, which are the leading cause of non-human-induced perimeter outages in industrial and agricultural settings.

2.3 Warranty Protection through Tooling Compliance

A significant commercial risk is the voiding of the 5-year manufacturer warranty due to improper installation techniques. The "Quick Connect" system relies on a specific mechanical crimp to ensure a watertight seal.

Mandatory tooling compliance requirements to maintain 5-year warranty validity.
Item Requirement Risk of Non-Compliance
Approved Tooling PPL-TOOL-CRIMP Water ingress at the connection point; warranty voidance.
Termination Kits PPL-ACC-TERM Corrosion of internal conductors; end-of-line failure.
Procurement Mandate — Crimp Tool

Procurement must verify that the installation contractor has been supplied with, or has purchased, the PPL-TOOL-CRIMP for every 500 m of perimeter deployment to ensure consistent termination quality.

Section 4

4. Configuration Rules & Bill of Materials (BOM) Logic

To ensure commercial accuracy and engineering performance, all Bills of Materials (BOM) must be constructed according to the following authoritative rules.

4.1 The 1 000 m Power Rule

A single Power Pack (PPL-PP-24V-STD) is rated to supply a maximum linear run of 1 000 m. For perimeters exceeding this length, procurement must divide the site into multiple zones, each with its own Power Pack. This prevents excessive voltage drop and ensures that the sensing asset remains operational across the entire perimeter.

Rule — Power Pack Zone Limit

1 Power Pack per 1 000 m (or per independent zone).

Commercial Implication — Redundancy

Multiple Power Packs increase system redundancy. If one zone is compromised, the remainder of the perimeter remains illuminated and monitored.

4.2 LED Spacing and Module Calculation

The standard spacing for perimeter illumination is 4 m between modules. This spacing provides the uniformity required for modern CCTV analytics to function effectively without dark spots.

Formula
LED Module Quantity Calculation
N = L / S
  • N Number of LED modules required
  • L Total linear perimeter length (metres)
  • S Selected spacing (standard = 4 m)
Example: For a 600 m perimeter at 4 m spacing:
N = 600 / 4 = 150 modules

4.3 Service Slack and Cable Over-Provisioning

Procurement must account for the physical realities of the installation environment. Cable runs are rarely perfectly linear; they must navigate corners, vertical drops at the Power Pack, and service loops at gates.

Requirement — Service Slack

Procurement must include a 5 % to 10 % "service slack" in the total cable order.

Example — Cable Order Calculation

A 600 m perimeter requires a minimum order of 650 m of PPL-CBL-UV-RR.

Section 6

6. Tender Referencing & Cross-Document Requirements

When the Perion system is included in high-stakes tender submissions (RFPs/RFQs), procurement must ensure that the bid is technically defensible and commercially attractive.

6.1 Positioning for Tender Evaluation Committees

Tender committees often evaluate systems based on the following three pillars: Technical Compliance, Commercial Value, and Lifecycle Reliability. Level 3 documentation supports these pillars as follows:

  1. Technical Compliance: Refer to Level 2 (TDS) for specific performance tables (Electrical, Optical, Mechanical). Level 3 ensures that the quoted BOM exactly matches the engineering design.
  2. Commercial Value (CAPEX/OPEX): Highlight the sensing asset ROI. Specifically, quantify the savings from the lack of trenching. Compared to 12 m high-mast floodlights, Perion reduces the civil cost component of the project by up to 70 % in linear perimeters.
  3. Lifecycle Reliability: Reference the 5-year warranty and the 100 000-hour L70 life expectancy of the LED modules. Emphasise that the use of PPL-CBL-UV-RR (rodent-repellent) cable reduces the probability of unplanned maintenance outages.

6.2 Mandatory Tender Appendix Documents

A successful procurement package for a Perion system should include the following extracts from the master system:

  • From Level 1: The Executive Summary (System Definition).
  • From Level 2: The Full Technical Specification Tables.
  • From Level 4: ISO 9001:2015 Manufacturing Declaration and Safety Standards (UL/CE/IEC).
  • From Level 6: Warranty and Support Statements (Internal reference).
Section 7

7. Electrical Zoning and Maintenance Infrastructure

The commercial viability of a large-scale perimeter system depends on its "Zone Integrity." If a single cable interruption takes down the entire 5 km perimeter, the system has failed as an infrastructure asset.

7.1 Zoning Rules for Procurement

Procurement must work with engineering to define the "Zone Map." Each zone must be commercially independent, meaning it has its own Power Pack and Perimeter-Lock cable-continuity sensor interface.

Rule — Zone Length Limit

No electrical zone may exceed 1 000 m.

Rule — High-Risk Zone Separation

High-risk zones (e.g., fuel depots, sensitive material stores) should be zoned separately to ensure maximum redundancy.

The transition to Perion's low-voltage perimeter illumination is fundamentally a financial decision. The following table provides the procurement-grade narrative for ROI justification.

Financial cost-avoidance comparison — Traditional High-Mast Lighting vs Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure.
Cost Driver Traditional High-Mast Lighting Perion Linear Perimeter Illumination Infrastructure Commercial Impact

8.1 "Lighting as a Sensor" — Third-Order Insights

The true value of the Perion system is not just the illumination it provides, but the data it generates for the security ecosystem. When a cable is interrupted, the relay triggers the nearest CCTV camera to zoom into that specific zone preset. This active response capability reduces the human capital required for perimeter monitoring, as security personnel only need to respond to verified alerts rather than monitoring static feeds for hours.

This efficiency gain provides measurable operational benefits that procurement should highlight to executive sign-off committees.

Section 9

9. Procurement Execution Checklist

To ensure that the "Technical Truth" of the Perion system is preserved during the purchasing cycle, the following checklist must be completed before the issuance of any Purchase Order (PO).

Pre-Purchase Order (PO) Verification Checklist All 7 items must be verified before PO issuance
  1. Verify Spacing: Does the number of LED modules match the perimeter length divided by the specified spacing (default 4 m)?
  2. Verify Power Packs: Is there at least one Power Pack for every 1 000 m of cable?
  3. Verify Cable Type: Is PPL-CBL-UV-RR specified to ensure rodent and UV protection?
  4. Verify Integration: Is a PPL-INT-RELAY included for every zone requiring alarm monitoring?
  5. Verify Tooling: Does the BOM include the mandatory PPL-TOOL-CRIMP?
  6. Verify Spares: Has a 5 % contingency buffer been added for modules and cable slack?
  7. Verify End-of-Line: Is there a PPL-ACC-TERM kit for every cable run?
